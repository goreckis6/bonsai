## Bunny Ears Cactus

### Botanical Classification
Bunny Ears Cactus belongs to the Cactaceae family, genus Opuntia, and is classified as Opuntia microdasys. It is a member of the subfamily Opuntioideae, characterized by flattened photosynthetic stems (cladodes) rather than traditional cylindrical ribs. The species exhibits a diploid chromosome count (2n = 22), a factor contributing to its relatively swift vegetative propagation compared with polyploid relatives.

### Native Habitat and Adaptations
Indigenous to the semi‑arid regions of central Mexico, this cactus evolved under intense solar radiation, periodic drought, and nutrient‑poor lithic soils. Its epidermal mucilage reduces transpiration, while the dense array of minute glochids—soft, barbed spines—deflect herbivory and create a reflective surface that diminishes leaf temperature. These adaptations underscore its suitability for low‑resource interior environments.

## Bunny Ears Cactus

### Optimal Growing Medium
A commercial cactus mix may serve as a baseline, yet a custom blend affords greater control over moisture dynamics. Incorporate 25 % washed river rock to further improve macro‑porosity, and blend in a thin layer (≈5 %) of fine charcoal to absorb excess salts. Prior to planting, sterilize the medium at 180 °F for 30 minutes to eradicate latent pathogens that could compromise delicate cladodes.

### Light, Water, and Temperature
Bunny Ears Cactus thrives under 10,000–12,000 lux of bright, filtered light. Supplemental LED grow lights (6500 K) can compensate for latitude‑related deficits, delivered 12 hours per day during winter dormancy. Watering should follow the “soak‑dry” cycle: saturate the substrate, then allow it to desiccate fully for 7–10 days before the next application. Temperature tolerance ranges from 50 °F (10 °C) to 90 °F (32 °C); however, sustained exposure below 45 °F (7 °C) precipitates etiolation and chlorosis.

## Maintenance and Health

### Common Pests
The principal insect concerns include mealybugs (Pseudococcus spp.) and spider mites (Tetranychidae). Early detection through routine leaf‑underside inspection permits targeted intervention with horticultural oil at a 2 % dilution. Beneficial predatory mites can be introduced as a biological control, establishing a sustainable equilibrium within the micro‑ecosystem.

### Disease Management
Root rot, predominantly caused by Phytophthora spp., emerges from over‑watering or poorly draining media. Preventative measures consist of soil moisture monitoring via a calibrated hygrometer, maintaining substrate water potential above –0.5 MPa only during active growth. Should rot develop, excise affected cladodes with sterilized tools and repot the specimen into fresh, pathogen‑free substrate.

## FAQ

### How often should I fertilize Bunny Ears Cactus?
Fertilization is recommended during the active growth phase, typically from March through June. Apply a balanced, low‑nitrogen liquid fertilizer (NPK 10‑10‑10) at one‑quarter the manufacturer’s strength, once every six weeks. Excess nitrogen can induce elongation and weaken the structural integrity of the cladodes.

### Can Bunny Ears Cactus survive in low‑light conditions?
While the plant tolerates moderate shade, prolonged low‑light exposure reduces chlorophyll production, leading to etiolation and sparse spine development. If natural light is insufficient, supplement with full‑spectrum LED lighting to deliver a minimum of 2,000 lux for 12‑hour periods.

### What is the best method for propagating Bunny Ears Cactus?
Vegetative propagation via cladode cuttings yields the highest success rate. Use a sterilized, sharp blade to remove a healthy segment, allow the wound to callous for 24–48 hours, then plant the cut end into a pre‑moistened cactus mix. Maintain high humidity (≈70 %) and indirect light until root emergence, typically within three weeks.

### Is it safe to keep Bunny Ears Cactus around pets?
The glochids on Bunny Ears Cactus are mildly irritating to skin and can cause discomfort if ingested by small animals. It is advisable to position the plant out of reach of curious pets, or select a barrier potting container that limits direct contact.

### How can I encourage a more compact growth habit?
Compactness is achieved by regulating light intensity and water frequency. Expose the plant to bright, indirect light and limit irrigation to the “soak‑dry” schedule described earlier. Pruning excess peripheral cladodes during early spring also redirects energy toward denser central growth.
