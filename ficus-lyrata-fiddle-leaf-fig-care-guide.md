## Ficus Lyrata Fiddle Leaf Fig Care Guide
The foundational step in any successful cultivation program is understanding the plant’s native ecology. In its rainforest understory, Ficus lyrata enjoys dappled sunlight, high humidity, and a nutrient‑rich humus layer. Translating these parameters indoors requires deliberate manipulation of light, moisture, and substrate.

### Light Requirements
Position the fig near an east‑ or west‑facing window where it receives morning or late‑afternoon sun. Aim for 4–6 hours of bright, indirect illumination daily. Too much direct exposure will manifest as bleached veins, while insufficient light leads to leggy stems and leaf drop.

#### Direct vs Indirect Light
Direct rays should be filtered through sheer curtains or reflected off a light‑diffusing surface. Indirect light can be amplified with sheer sheers or a north‑facing window that offers consistent, moderate intensity. Supplemental LED grow lights calibrated to 6500 K can bridge seasonal deficits.

### Temperature and Humidity
Maintain ambient temperatures between 65–75 °F (18–24 °C). Sudden drops below 55 °F stress vascular tissue and trigger leaf curl. Humidity levels of 50–60 % are ideal; employ a tabletop humidifier or a pebble tray during winter heating cycles.

## Ficus Lyrata Fiddle Leaf Fig Care Guide
Addressing the plant’s water dynamics is essential for preventing root rot and leaf edge browning. The succulent‑like petiole stores limited moisture; however, the species is not drought tolerant and reacts quickly to moisture fluctuations.

### Watering Schedule
Water thoroughly until excess drains from the bottom holes. Allow the soil profile to dry to approximately 2 inches before the next application. In winter, reduce frequency by 30 % to mirror natural rainfall patterns.

#### Signs of Over‑watering
Yellowing leaves, soft stems, and a musty odor indicate hypoxic root conditions. Immediate corrective action includes repotting in a fresh, well‑draining mix and adjusting watering intervals.

#### Signs of Under‑watering
Crisp leaf margins, drooping fronds, and a slight wilt signal insufficient moisture. Increase watering volume and consider misting to raise ambient humidity.

## Expert Tips for Ficus Lyrata Fiddle Leaf Fig Care Guide
Seasonal pruning aligns growth with light availability, encouraging compact form and preventing top‑heavy imbalance. Use sanitized, sharp pruning shears to remove dead or damaged leaves at the base of the petiole.

### Fertilization
Apply a balanced, water‑soluble fertilizer (NPK 20‑20‑20) at half strength monthly during the active growth period (spring–early fall). Suspend feeding in winter when metabolic activity slows.

### Soil Composition
A mix of 40 % peat moss, 30 % perlite, 20 % pine bark fines, and 10 % worm castings delivers both moisture retention and aeration. Adjust proportions for local climate; arid regions benefit from increased perlite.

## Light Requirements
Ensuring consistent light exposure mitigates etiolation and promotes sturdy stem development. Rotate the plant a quarter turn every two weeks to equalize leaf orientation.

### Supplemental Lighting
LED panels positioned 12–18 inches above the canopy provide 2500–3000 lumens per square foot. Operate on a 12‑hour photoperiod to simulate tropical day length.

## Watering Schedule
Precision in watering safeguards against both stem collapse and fungal intrusion. Employ a moisture meter inserted at a 2‑inch depth to verify dryness before irrigation.

### Automated Irrigation
Drip‑line systems with timers can deliver measured volumes, reducing human error. Calibrate flow rates to deliver 10 ml per minute, adjusting for pot size.

## Soil and Repotting
Repotting stimulates root expansion and prevents pot‑bound restriction. Choose a container with drainage holes no larger than ¼ inch to avoid excessive water loss.

### Repotting Procedure
1. Gently loosen the root ball and trim any circling roots.  
2. Place a layer of fresh mix at the pot’s base.  
3. Position the plant centrally, filling gaps with soil and firming lightly.  
4. Water thoroughly to settle the medium.

## Pests and Diseases
A vigilant inspection routine catches infestations before systemic damage occurs. Use a soft brush or cotton swab dipped in isopropyl alcohol to remove mites and mealybugs.

### Common Pests
- **Spider mites**: Fine webbing on undersides; treat with neem oil.  
- **Mealybugs**: Cotton‑white clusters; apply horticultural oil.  
- **Scale insects**: Hard shells; prune heavily infested branches.

### Fungal Issues
Leaf spot fungi emerge in overly humid conditions. Increase airflow, reduce watering frequency, and apply copper‑based fungicide per label instructions.

## Propagation Techniques
Vegetative propagation preserves genetic fidelity and accelerates collection expansion. Stem cuttings rooted in water or moist substrate develop robust root systems within 4–6 weeks.

### Water Propagation
Submerge a 6‑inch cutting in filtered water, changing the medium every two days. Once roots reach 2 inches, transplant into a peat‑perlite mix.

### Soil Propagation
Dip the cut end in rooting hormone, plant in a moist seed‑starting mix, and cover with a clear plastic dome to retain humidity.

## FAQ

### How often should I fertilize my fiddle leaf fig?
Fertilize monthly during spring through early autumn using a balanced, water‑soluble fertilizer at half the recommended strength. Suspend feeding in winter when growth naturally slows.

### What is the best way to increase humidity for my Ficus lyrata?
Place the pot on a pebble tray filled with water, ensuring the pot sits above the water line to avoid root rot. Alternatively, operate a tabletop humidifier set to 50‑60 % relative humidity.

### Can I prune my fiddle leaf fig to control size?
Yes. Prune in early spring using clean, sharp shears, removing only dead, diseased, or excessively long branches. This encourages a bushier habit and prevents top‑heavy instability.

### Why are the leaves turning yellow at the edges?
Yellowing margins typically indicate inconsistent watering—either over‑watering leading to root stress or under‑watering causing nutrient uptake issues. Adjust your watering schedule and verify soil moisture before each irrigation.

### How do I know when to repot my fiddle leaf fig?
When the plant shows roots emerging from drainage holes, the soil surface dries rapidly after watering, or the foliage appears cramped, it is time to repot. Aim for a pot two sizes larger than the current one.
